Abstract
Background: Automated astrocyte quantification in human immunohistochemistry (IHC) is hin-
dered by stain variability and dense clustering. Standard Transformers often miss faint somata due
to agnostic initialization, while CNNs struggle with overlapping instances.
Methods: We propose a Heatmap-Seeded Query Transformer coupling candidate sufficiency with
global competitive assignment. Queries initialized from CNN-derived spatial peaks capture faint tar-
gets and are refined via a cross-scale Transformer decoder. We evaluated the model on ALDH1L1- and
GFAP-stained human tissue (0.35–0.50 µm/px) against Faster R-CNN, YOLOv11, and Deformable
DETR using COCO-style metric under the Olar et al. evaluation protocol, bootstrap-resampled FROC
analysis, and targeted stratifications for local contrast and pair-separation recall.
Results: Our method improved overall Average Precision (AP) across the vast majority of test
cohorts, including a nearly 19% relative increase in high-resolution ALDH1L1 images. Crucially,
stratified evaluations revealed significant improvements in localizing low-contrast targets and resolving
densely crowded cell pairs without relying on heuristic filtering. FROC analysis confirmed superior
sensitivity at clinically relevant low false-positive rates.
Conclusions: Integrating spatial priors with global reasoning effectively mitigates the sensitivity-
precision trade-off. This yields a robust detector for astrocyte soma localization resilient to stain and
resolution shifts, serving as a highly reliable upstream component for quantitative digital pathology
workflows.

Background
Astrocytes constitute a fundamental class of glial
cells within the central nervous system, charac-
terized by their complex stellate morphology and
essential roles in supporting neuronal function,
regulating cerebral blood flow, and modulating
synaptic activity [1, 2]. In response to a wide spec-
trum of neurological insults, ranging from acute
1
trauma and ischemia to chronic neurodegenera-
tive conditions like Alzheimer’s disease, astrocytes
undergo a process known as reactive gliosis [3–5].
This state is marked by cellular hypertrophy, the
upregulation of intermediate filaments, and alter-
ations in astrocytic density and spatial distribu-
tion. While evaluating fine morphological changes
requires precise cell segmentation, accurate local-
ization and robust enumeration of astrocytes serve
as the fundamental first step for evaluating reac-
tive gliosis. Quantifying these population-level
dynamics, such as absolute cell counts and spa-
tial clustering patterns, provides critical insight
into disease progression and the evaluation of
therapeutic efficacy. While genetically encoded
fluorescent reporters in transgenic mice offer pris-
tine visualization for mechanistic studies, clinical
neuropathology and archival research rely heavily
on immunohistochemistry (IHC) of human brain
tissue [6]. Unlike controlled animal models, human
IHC da ta inherently suffers from significant chal-
lenges, including variable staining intensity, non-
specific background noise from DAB chromogens,
and a lack of channel separation. These factors
make robust automated quantification a persistent
bottleneck in translational research.
Reliable identification of astrocytes in bright-
field microscopy is further confounded by two
primary methodological barriers: stain-dependent
appearance shifts and dense cellular clustering.
Traditional object detectors based on Convolu-
tional Neural Networks, such as Faster R-CNN
and YOLO, have revolutionized biomedical image
analysis but face inherent limitations in this con-
text [7–11]. These models typically rely on local
receptive fields and heuristic non-maximum sup-
pression (NMS) to filter predictions. In dense
astrocytic networks where processes from neigh-
boring cells intertwine, heuristic NMS often erro-
neously suppresses true positives or fails to sep-
arate overlapping somata. Consequently, CNN-
based approaches frequently struggle to balance
sensitivity and precision when handling the intri-
cate morphologies of glia.
More recently, the advent of Detection Trans-
formers (DETR) has introduced a paradigm shift
by leveraging global attention mechanisms for one-
to-one set matching [12, 13]. Unlike CNN-based
detectors that generate thousands of redundant
anchors requiring aggressive filtering, Transformer
architectures explicitly learn to suppress dupli-
cate predictions during training. However, stan-
dard Transformer architectures typically rely on
content-agnostic learned queries to initialize the
detection process. In the specific context of astro-
cyte detection, where targets can be extremely
small or faintly stained, these blind queries often
fail to locate valid objects during the initial train-
ing phases. This limitation, often referred to as the
candidate sufficiency problem, leads to slow con-
vergence and suboptimal recall for subtle morpho-
logical features [14–18]. Therefore, a significant
theoretical gap remains regarding how to simul-
taneously guarantee high recall for faint targets
while maintaining the global discriminative power
needed to resolve crowded instances.
Recent advancements in deep learning for med-
ical image analysis have explored various strate-
gies to enhance feature representation and small
object detection, including modern Transformer
and hybrid backbones [19–21] and self-supervised
pretraining paradigms [22–24]. In computational
pathology, stain variability and batch effects
remain major obstacles to model generalization,
motivating stain augmentation and domain gen-
eralization strategies [25–28]. For astrocyte quan-
tification, prior pipelines have leveraged deep
learning for detection/segmentation in fluorescent
micrographs [29] and for GFAP-immunolabeled
astrocyte detection in brightfield images [30, 31].
Drawing inspiration from these developments,
we hypothesize that the limitations of current
astrocyte detectors can be effectively addressed
by explicitly coupling spatial priors with global
reasoning. Specifically, by seeding Transformer
queries with high-confidence peaks derived from
a computationally efficient CNN heatmap (as
in heatmap-based detection and adaptive sam-
ple selection [32, 33]), we can ensure that even
faint somata are attended to, thereby solving the
issue of candidate sufficiency. Meanwhile, the sub-
sequent Transformer decoder can perform com-
petitive assignment to distinguish true cells from
background artifacts, effectively resolving issues
related to cellular crowding.
In
this
paper,
we
propose
and
validate
a Heatmap-Seeded Spatially Grounded Query
Transformer designed for robust astrocyte soma
detection. Unlike Deformable DETR variants that
learn query positions from scratch, our method
utilizes a heatmap-guided mechanism to generate
2
data-dependent anchors. These anchors serve as
explicit spatial priors for the transformer decoder,
allowing the model to focus on refining localization
rather than discovering objects from scratch. This
architecture effectively bridges the gap between
the high recall of heatmap-based methods and the
precision of transformer-based global assignment.
We posit that this mechanism effectively mitigates
the sensitivity-precision trade-off exacerbated by
domain shifts in human tissue. To validate this
central claim, we evaluate the model on a diverse
dataset of ALDH1L1 and GFAP stained human
brain tissue [34] across varying resolutions of 0.50
and 0.35 µm/px. The results demonstrate that
our heatmap-seeded mechanism consistently out-
performs established baselines, including Faster
R-CNN and Deformable DETR, yielding the most
significant gains in high-resolution cohorts where
candidate sufficiency is most critical. By providing
robust cell counting resilient to stain and resolu-
tion variability, this work holds great promise for
alleviating a key bottleneck in automated human
neuropathology.
Methods
Theoretical Framework and
Motivation
Automated
astrocyte
detection
in
brightfield
immunohistochemistry faces critical hurdles, pri-
marily the candidate sufficiency problem where
Deformable DETR-based methods with learned
queries fail to detect faint somata due to a lack
of spatial priors [12, 14–17]. Furthermore, the
crowding problem poses a significant challenge as
CNN-based detectors struggle to separate entan-
gled processes of adjacent cells, while domain
shifts caused by stain variability further com-
plicate robust quantification. To address these
challenges, we propose a hybrid architecture that
couples a CNN feature pyramid for local tex-
ture extraction with a Transformer decoder for
global competitive assignment. Crucially, rather
than relying on content-agnostic learned queries,
our method grounds the Transformer by seeding
it with high-confidence spatial peaks derived from
a center heatmap. This design ensures that com-
putation focuses on plausible somata, especially
faint ones, while the subsequent cross-attention
mechanism resolves scale uncertainty and dense
clustering.
Overall Architecture
As illustrated in Figure 1, the proposed detector
processes input images resized to 512 × 512 pix-
els and consists of three integrated components.
First, a ResNet-50 backbone extracts hierarchi-
cal features, into which a lightweight Transformer
block is inserted at the C4 stage to enhance long-
range dependency modeling. These features are
subsequently fused via a Feature Pyramid Net-
work to produce multi-scale maps P2, P3, and
P4 with strides of 4, 8, and 16, respectively. Sec-
ond, a projection head on the high-resolution
P2 map predicts a dense center heatmap, from
which local maxima are extracted to serve as
spatially grounded anchors. Third, a dynamic
query decoder samples feature vectors from P2 at
these anchor locations to initialize queries. These
queries are then refined through L layers of self-
attention and cross-attention over a multi-scale
memory constructed from the feature pyramid,
enabling the model to effectively handle scale
variations.

Optimization and Inference
We adopt a set-based training strategy using
bipartite Hungarian matching to assign predic-
tions to ground truths. The total loss L constitutes
a weighted sum of heatmap reconstruction and
detection losses:
L = whmLFocal(H, H⋆)+λclsLcls+λL1LL1+λiouLCIoU
where Lcls is the focal classification loss, LL1 is
the L1 regression loss, and LCIoU is the Complete
IoU loss [35]. The loss coefficients are empirically
balanced (whm = 2.0, λcls = 1.0, λL1 = 6.0, and
λiou = 2.0) to stabilize the learning dynamics.
This composite loss forces the model to pre-
dict exactly one bounding box per cell, effec-
tively teaching the Transformer decoder to sup-
press duplicates via global query competition.
During inference, bounding box coordinates are
decoded from the relative offsets. Crucially, the
Transformer successfully learns this global reason-
ing and one-to-one assignment, making standard
heuristic Non-Maximum Suppression theoretically
unnecessary. Detailed in Supplementary Table S1,
our ablation studies empirically confirm this archi-
tectural independence. The raw network operating
without any post-processing NMS handles dense
crowding efficiently and natively achieves highly
competitive performance. For example, on the
GFAP 05019 Cohort 2, the raw network yields an
impressive AP of 0.820.
Since our queries are dynamically seeded from
a continuous heatmap, highly irregular or hyper-
trophic somata can occasionally induce the CNN
backbone to generate split peaks, where a sin-
gle cell produces two highly adjacent candidate
seeds. To address these rare structural ambigui-
ties without aggressively deleting faint targets in
crowded regions, we apply a lightweight Soft-NMS
step configured with an IoU threshold of 0.5 and
a smoothing parameter σ of 0.5. We explicitly
emphasize that our model does not rely on this
algorithm to resolve crowding. As demonstrated in
the supplementary ablation, this residual cleanup
removes virtually zero initial predictions and pro-
vides only a marginal numerical gain, such as
shifting the AP from 0.820 to 0.821.

Data Preprocessing and
Augmentation
The model was evaluated on the dataset pro-
vided by Olar et al. [34]. To strictly adhere
to the backbone stride requirements, all input
image patches were resized from their original
dimensions to a fixed resolution of 512 × 512 pix-
els. To improve robustness against the variability
of IHC staining, such as illumination and con-
trast differences, we applied a comprehensive data
augmentation pipeline using the Albumentations
library. This pipeline included geometric transfor-
mations like random horizontal or vertical flips
and affine transformations. Furthermore, we incor-
porated pixel-level augmentations including ran-
dom brightness and contrast adjustment, CLAHE,
random gamma correction, and Gaussian blur.
These augmentations simulate the domain shifts
encountered in clinical microscopy, enhancing the
model’s generalization capabilities.
Implementation Details
Two
separate
models
were
trained:
one
for
ALDH1L1-stained images and one for GFAP-
stained images, allowing the networks to spe-
cialize in the distinct morphological features of
each marker. The framework was implemented in
PyTorch. Optimization was performed using the
AdamW optimizer with an initial learning rate
of 2 × 10−4 and weight decay of 4 × 10−4. We
employed a batch size of 4 and trained for 50
epochs on a single NVIDIA A100 GPU. A learning
rate warm-up was applied for the first 15 epochs.
Training times were approximately 2 hours for
the ALDH1L1 model and 3 hours for the GFAP
model.
To evaluate the computational feasibility of
our approach, we also measured inference effi-
ciency, including throughput and peak GPU mem-
ory consumption. Operating on 512 × 512 res-
olution patches with a batch size of 4, our
model achieved a throughput of approximately
26.5 frames per second (FPS) with a peak mem-
ory footprint of 1.36 GB. While fully convolu-
tional architectures like YOLOv11 and Faster
R-CNN are inherently faster, our hybrid architec-
ture proved to be both faster and more memory-
efficient than the Deformable DETR baseline
(20.9 FPS, 1.47 GB). Given the offline nature
of whole-slide histological analysis, this compu-
tational profile is highly acceptable, particularly
when weighed against the substantial improve-
ments achieved in faint target localization and
crowded soma separation. The detailed compu-
tational efficiency comparison across all baseline
models is provided in the Supplementary Material
(Table S2).
Evaluation Metrics and Baselines
We report COCO-style AP/AR under the Olar et
al. evaluation protocol, primarily Average Preci-
sion (AP) and Average Recall (AR) averaged over
IoU thresholds from 0.05 to 0.50. These metrics
rely on standard IoU-based bounding box match-
ing to facilitate direct comparison with the pri-
mary baseline. Complementary to this, we utilize
Free-Response ROC (FROC) analysis to evalu-
ate the sensitivity versus false positives per image
(FPPI) trade-off. In accordance with the origi-
nal evaluation protocol for this dataset, FROC
matching relies on a center-in-box criterion: a
true positive (TP) is recorded if the center of a
predicted bounding box falls strictly within the
boundaries of a ground-truth box. FROC curves
were generated via bootstrap resampling with B =
200 to provide 95% confidence intervals.

Datasets and Annotations
We evaluate our method on the publicly available
astrocyte histology dataset introduced by Olar
et al. [34]. This dataset consists of immunohis-
tochemically stained human brain tissue samples
with annotations of astrocyte cell bodies. In total,
8730 image patches (each 500×500 pixels) were
extracted from whole-slide images across 16 slides
from 8 patients . The dataset is stratified into two
distinct staining conditions to capture morpholog-
ical heterogeneity. The first subset comprises 3,705
patches stained for ALDH1L1, a pan-astrocyte
marker that primarily labels the cell body [6]; this
subset is partitioned into 3,281 patches for train-
ing/validation and 424 for testing. The second
subset consists of 5,025 patches stained for GFAP,
which highlights astrocytic cytoskeletal filaments
[6], divided into 4,593 for training/validation and
432 for testing.
These patches were acquired at two differ-
ent digital scanning resolutions: a set at 0.5019
µm/pixel and another at 0.3557 µm/pixel .
Accordingly, the dataset is divided into three
test cohorts reflecting different sources and imag-
ing conditions: 1. Test 05019 Cohort 1: Held-out
patches at 0.5019 µm/px from a subset of patients
(brightfield images, either ALDH1L1 or GFAP
stained). 2. Test 05019 Cohort 2: Additional
held-out patches at 0.5019 µm/px from different
slides/patients, to assess variability. 3. Test 03557
Cohort: Patches at the finer resolution of 0.3557
µm/px (includes both stains).
All images are RGB brightfield micrographs
with DAB chromogen staining. Each astrocyte in
a patch was annotated by experts with a bound-
ing box delineating the cell body extent. Multiple
annotators contributed, and a consensus annota-
tion was established for ground truth. The test
sets are fully independent of training data, rep-
resenting unseen patients and slides to evaluate
generalization. We use the same fixed train/test
split defined in the original dataset to enable
direct comparison with the published baseline.
Additionally, we set aside approximately 10% of
the train as a validation to help us identify the
optimal model. A detailed quantitative breakdown
of the splits and annotations is provided in Table
1.

To evaluate whether the increased recall comes

Discussion
The results presented in this study substanti-
ate our central hypothesis: effective automation
of human neuropathology requires bridging the
gap between local textural sensitivity and global
structural reasoning. By outperforming the Faster
R-CNN baseline [34] and the Deformable DETR
architecture [13] in the vast majority of test
cohorts, our Heatmap-Seeded Query Transformer
demonstrates that explicitly coupling spatial pri-
ors with global attention provides a robust solu-
tion to the challenges of stain variability and
resolution shifts inherent in clinical samples.
The most significant performance improve-
ments, particularly the substantial increase in
AP observed in the low-contrast subsets, pro-
vide empirical support for the mechanism of can-
didate sufficiency. Standard Transformer detec-
tors typically rely on learned queries that are
content-agnostic at initialization. As evidenced by
the comparative analysis against the Deformable
DETR baseline (Table 4), this blind search strat-
egy often fails to converge on faintly stained
somata. In contrast, our approach utilizes the
heatmap to inject strong spatial priors into
the decoding process. This ensures that even
severely degraded morphological features trigger a
query, effectively eliminating the cold-start prob-
lem inherent in standard DETR-like architectures.
Consequently, the model captures dim or border-
localized cells that are systematically missed by
standard Transformers, confirming that explicit
spatial grounding is indispensable for detecting
biological targets lacking strong visual promi-
nence.
Regarding the challenge of cellular crowding,
our stratified evaluation on dense subsets and
the remarkably high Pair-Separation Recall (PSR)
confirm that the mechanism of global competitive
assignment effectively isolates adjacent instances.
CNN detectors such as Faster R-CNN and YOLO
[7–10] rely heavily on heuristic non-maximum
suppression (NMS). In dense astrocytic networks
where processes intertwine, heuristic NMS suffers
from severe anchor entanglement and erroneously
suppresses true positives. Our ablation studies
empirically confirm that the Transformer’s self-
attention mechanism successfully models the rela-
tionships between overlapping queries to resolve
this crowding natively. The supplementary abla-
tion demonstrates that the lightweight Soft-NMS
applied during inference removes virtually zero
initial predictions, acting merely as a residual
score-decay filter for rare split peaks. Thus, the
primary burden of duplicate removal is handled
by the learnable one-to-one matching objective,
resulting in a cleaner and more robust detection
set compared to purely anchor-based approaches.
We further observed that the differential gains
between ALDH1L1 and GFAP cohorts reflect the
distinct morphological challenges posed by each
marker. The baseline models already achieved
high recall on certain GFAP slides, likely due to
the prominent, high-contrast filament structures
that are easily captured by convolutional filters.
However, our model still delivered critical incre-
mental gains in precision, primarily by reducing
false positives in regions with non-specific DAB
background staining. In contrast, the substan-
tial improvements in ALDH1L1 detection high-
light the necessity of the hybrid architecture for
identifying globular, low-contrast somata, which
require the contextual integration capabilities of
the Transformer to be reliably distinguished from
the neuropil background.Additionally, the pro-
nounced performance disparity observed between
GFAP 05019 Cohort 1 and Cohort 2 is intrinsi-
cally tied to the fundamental structural composi-
tion of the test sets. As outlined in our dataset
structure, Cohort 1 encapsulates a significantly
higher proportion of complex anatomical struc-
tures and sparse target distributions. Importantly,
this structural complexity directly elucidates why
the FROC curves for these two cohorts appear
highly comparable despite the substantial gap in

Despite these promising results, we acknowl-
edge critical areas for future investigation, partic-
ularly regarding model generalization and annota-
tion costs. A significant challenge in fully super-
vised histopathological object detection is the
prohibitive cost of expert annotation, which scales
poorly when generalizing to new laboratory envi-
ronments, different scanners, or novel immunohis-
tochemical markers. From a practical deployment
perspective, our pre-trained model can be applied
directly out-of-the-box to new clinical cohorts
that share similar staining protocols and scan-
ner characteristics. However, in the presence of
severe domain shifts—such as extreme variations
in DAB intensity, background chromogen pooling,
or novel tissue preparation techniques—we rec-
ommend employing lightweight fine-tuning with a
minimal set of in-domain annotations to recali-
brate the heatmap priors. To further mitigate this
annotation bottleneck and improve cross-domain
generalization, future work must explore weakly
supervised learning, active learning paradigms,
or self-supervised feature extraction to adapt the
model with minimal manual intervention. Finally,
the current framework focuses strictly on reliable
bounding-box localization and counting. Given
the complex branching morphology of astrocytes,
extending this upstream architecture to include a
downstream segmentation branch remains a nec-
essary step toward comprehensive morphometric
analysis.
Conclusions
This work establishes that incorporating heatmap-
seeded spatial priors into a Transformer detec-
tion pipeline effectively mitigates the sensitivity-
precision trade-off in astrocyte quantification. By
addressing the specific bottleneck of candidate
sufficiency for low-contrast targets and demon-
strating robust, highly competitive instance sep-
aration in densely crowded scenes, the proposed
framework advances the methodological standard
for automated analysis in human neuropathology.
These findings provide a scalable foundation for
future computational tools aimed at dissecting
glial function in health and disease.

Ablation Study on Post-Processing
Non-Maximum Suppression
To empirically validate our assertion that the
Transformer decoder naturally learns global com-
petitive assignment and does not rely on post-
processing heuristics to resolve crowded somata,
we conducted a comprehensive ablation study
removing the Soft-NMS step entirely. As detailed
in Supplementary Table S1, the Raw network
natively outputs a highly refined set of bounding
boxes with exceptional precision and recall across
all test cohorts.
When Soft-NMS (IoU threshold = 0.5, σ =
0.5) is applied to these raw predictions, it merely
functions as a soft score-decay filter. Conse-
quently, the performance gain is exceptionally
marginal, confirming that the Soft-NMS mecha-
nism acts strictly as a lightweight residual cleanup
for occasional heatmap split peaks, rather than a
primary solution for separating dense overlapping
instances.

Comparison with Originally
Published Baselines
In the main text, all comparative analyses strictly
utilize a rigorously retrained Faster R-CNN base-
line. This ensures that both our proposed model
and the baseline are evaluated under identical
environmental configurations and hyperparame-
ter settings, particularly for the newly introduced
local contrast and density stratifications. However,
for complete transparency and to facilitate direct
reference to the foundational dataset paper, Table
S3 presents a comparison between our Heatmap-
Seeded Query Transformer and the aggregate
Faster R-CNN metrics exactly as originally pub-
lished by Olar et al. [34].
As demonstrated, when compared against the
originally reported metrics, our proposed architec-
ture maintains a clear and consistent advantage
across all six independent test cohorts. Notably,
it overcomes the candidate sufficiency bottleneck
in the high-resolution ALDH1L1 03557 cohort
(improving AP from 0.540 to 0.646) and suc-
cessfully surpasses the published baseline even
in the sparse GFAP 05019 Cohort 1 (AP 0.323
vs. published 0.312). Overall, our model yields a
macro-average Average Precision (AP) of 0.699
compared to the published 0.651, further confirm-
ing the broad applicability of explicitly coupling
spatial priors with global attention.
Stratified Sample Size Distribution
To provide transparent context for the strati-
fied evaluation metrics (Local Contrast AP, Dense
AP, and Pair-Separation Recall) presented in the
main text, Table S4 details the absolute num-
ber of ground-truth annotations falling into these
specific sub-categories across all six test cohorts.
Notably, the target distribution varies signifi-
cantly across different datasets. For instance, the
sparse GFAP 05019 Cohort 1 and Cohort 2 con-
tain a mere 8 and 7 dense instances (Dj ≥2),
respectively. Similarly, the highly crowded pairs
(∥cj −ck∥2 ≤τ) in these specific subsets are
extremely scarce (25 and 57 pairs, respectively).
This extreme sample scarcity directly explains
the low Dense AP and the numerical instability
observed in these specific GFAP subsets across
all evaluated architectures. It further confirms
that the performance degradation in these sparse
scenarios is a byproduct of the skewed dataset
composition rather than algorithmic failure. In
contrast, the robust sample sizes in the dense
ALDH1L1 03557 cohort (138 dense instances and
240 crowded pairs) provide highly confident sta-
tistical validation of our model’s robust instance
separation capabilities.

Detailed Failure Mode Analysis
While our heatmap-guided architecture demon-
strates significant improvements in candidate suf-
ficiency and dense separation, it is not completely
immune to errors when faced with extreme histo-
logical artifacts or severe biological irregularities.
To ensure a transparent evaluation of the model’s
limitations, we provide a qualitative analysis of
representative failure modes in Figure S1. The pri-
mary sources of error manifest in three distinct
phenomena. First, boundary truncation frequently
leads to false negatives. Astrocytes located pre-
cisely on the edge of the image patch are often
severely truncated (Figure S1a, blue arrows). This
loss of vital morphological context and the dis-
ruption of the central soma structure prevent the
CNN backbone from generating a confident spatial
peak in the heatmap, leading to missed detections.
Second, although our model excels at separat-
ing typical dense clusters, extremely faint somata
that are tightly entangled with adjacent, strongly
stained cells can still occasionally confound the
matching process (Figure S1b, yellow arrows). In
such extreme cases, the model may either erro-
neously merge a faint cell with its neighbor or
artificially split a highly irregular, fragmented
soma into two bounding boxes. Finally, immuno-
histochemical processing can sometimes produce
non-specific DAB chromogen pooling or tissue
folding artifacts. When these artifacts exhibit
structural and intensity profiles that closely mimic
true astrocytic somata, the model may incor-
rectly assign them high confidence scores, result-
ing in false positives (Figure S1b, orange arrows).
Addressing these edge cases—potentially through
the integration of broader contextual windows
to resolve boundary truncations or advanced
artifact-rejection modules—represents a valuable
direction for future refinement.
